Cryptography in the first and second world wars



Although in the First World War the encryption of messages was frequently used by Germany and the allies, one of the most important events where cryptography is seen as the protagonist was when the decryption services of the English Admiralty Room 40, intercepted a telegram that the then German Foreign Minister Arthur Zimmerman sent on January 16, 1917 to Count Johann Heinrich Andreas, German ambassador to the United States, who in turn had to forward that telegram to the German ambassador in Mexico, the importance This message consisted of the fact that it evidenced Germany's intentions to ally with Mexico in the event that the United States decided not to remain neutral and that it also intended to initiate an unrestricted campaign of submarine warfare. When the content of the telegram reached the hands of the President of the United States, he decided to enter the war despite the firmness with which he had previously refused to participate.


Already in the second world war, it was different, cryptography took on even more importance. Friedman, for example, led the SIS team of cryptanalysts who cracked the Japanese crypto machine (named Purple) in August 1940 after 18 months of hard work. In fact, due to this effort, Friedman had a mental breakdown for which it was necessary to confine him to a mental hospital for three months. There is also the famous Enigma Machine, which was the official cryptographic machine of the government of Germany and although most of us know the story of director Morten Tyldum's film `` The Enigma Code '', there is also a very interesting story and equal or more important to it. The story that the same version of the English and it is the work that the Poles did for years to decipher the messages of their neighbor through a not so exact but very functional version of an enigma machine, the importance of their work lies in the fact that After the German invasion of Poland all the information that the Poles collected passed into the hands of the government school of encryption and encryption in England and later into the hands of Alan Turing and the entire team at Bletchley Park who in turn created `` Christopher "a machine predecessor of computers that decrypted German messages.
